## A  Look  Back  at Game Hardware

It may have taken some time, but it seems like the weather has finally made up its mind about whether to give up on the cold or not, and leapt into summertime instead.

Our meeting takes place at the usual time and date, back in the usual 'group  study'  room.  I  will  bring back the Vampire-fied Amiga 600. I managed  to  find  my  way  around the  problem  I  was  having  at  the meeting last  month,  where  games loaded via WHDload used a key to cancel  to  Workbench  which  was not  available  on  the  short-form A600 keyboard. I'll go into how that issue was fixed at the meeting. I'll also show off classic Amiga DOOMclone  GLOOM, (actually Gloom Deluxe) which runs nicely on the Vampire.

As I gain more experience with the system,  I've  learned  to  moderate my  hype  a  bit.  The  Vampire  definitely runs significantly faster than a 68060 or slower system, but it's still  a  bit  much  to  expect  silkysmooth high-res 3D gaming that a PPC or Intel system might give you. I might get better performance out of the faster core versions, but with my past experience, I'm playing it safe for now.

The  lock-out  enabled  Nintendo  to  exert  control  on  the  cartridge production and market, (perhaps  draconian  control)  and game producers had to play ball if they wanted to get on the system at all (or circumvent the lockout somehow, and probably get sued).

No  matter  what  others  thought about  it,  Nintendo's  approach  became  the  model  for  nearly  everyone's  game  console  markets  since then. In Japan, Nintendo was moving to the 'Disk System' a proprietary  floppy  disk-based  expansion for  their  Famicom,  which  boosted the memory and specs of the base unit somewhat, running games from the disks, and allowing game data to be saved to the disk as well. Several NES games we know today got their start as disk games in Japan,  adapted  because  the  disk  expansion never made it to the US. I don't know exactly why, but in any case the technology of the cartridge games  themselves  were  growing, to the point where the disk system wasn't a big advantage, with hardware included to enable new capabilities, larger games, or battery-backed saving. This is much like Atari and some third parties had already done for the 2600. Additions to the cartridges and clever  programming  made  it  possible to circumvent the console's 4K limit  thru  'bank  switching,'  bringing us games at sizes of 8K, 16K, 32K, and  even  beyond.
